Vector space

Fourier series can be thought of as an infinite dimensional vector space, where the basis are sinusoid harmonics. An inner product in the vector space of functions is defined analoguosly to its finite dimensional counterpart. $$ \langle f,g \rangle=\int f(x)g(x)dx $$ The analogy is clear when the integral is aproximated as a finite sum of products of sampled values of the two functions. In the case of Fourier series, because we are only interested in functions with a period of $T$, the inner product is defined as $$ \langle f,g \rangle= \int_0^T f(x)g(x)dx $$

Orthogonality

Because functions of interest are periodic with period $T$, so are the sines and cosines constituting the basis (otherwise their sum wouldn't be periodic with perioud $T$). \[ \sin\left(n\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right), \ \cos\left(n\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right), \ n \in \{0,1,2,\ldots\} \] Sine and cosine harmonics form a basis because they are orthogonal. The notion of orthogonality is "adopted" by analogy from finite dimensional vector spaces, i.e. inner product of zero. For simplicity let $T= 2\pi$. \begin{align} \int_0^{2\pi} \sin(nx)\cos(mx)dx & = 0, \ m,n = \{0,1,2,\ldots\} \\ \int_0^{2\pi} \cos(nx)\cos(mx)dx & = 0, \ m,n = \{1,2,\ldots\}, \ m\neq n \\ \int_0^{2\pi} \sin(nx)\sin(mx)dx & = 0, \ m,n = \{1,2,\ldots\}, \ m\neq n \end{align} Another function that also constitutes the basis is the constant function $1$. And is also orthogonal to the other basis components: \begin{align} \int_0^{2\pi} 1\sin(nx)dx & = 0, \ n = \{1,2,\ldots\} \\ \int_0^{2\pi} 1\cos(nx)dx & = 0, \ n = \{1,2,\ldots\} \end{align}

Calculating coefficients

The Fourier series is defined as in the introductory chapter, (For simplicity, let $T=2\pi$) \begin{equation} f(t) = A_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}S_n \sin(nt) + C_n \cos(nt) \end{equation} The coefficients $A_0, S_n$ and $C_n$ are projections of $f(t)$ onto the basis functions $1, \sin(nt)$ and $\cos(nt)$.

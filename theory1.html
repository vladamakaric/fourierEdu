Periodic functions

A function \(f(x)\) is periodic if a constant \(T\) exists such that for all \(x\): The smallest such \(T\) is called the fundamental period \(T_1\). The fundamental frequency \(f_1\) is defined as the reciprocal of \(T_1\) $$f_1 = \frac{1}{T_1}$$ A harmonic of the function \(f(x)\) is every function \(g(x)\) with a fundamental frequency that is an integer multiple of \(T\), \( f(2x), f(3x), \ldots, f(nx) \). Every harmonic is also periodic with the period \(T\). $$f(nx) = f(n(x + T)) = f(nx + nT)$$ Shifting a function does not effect its periodicity. $$ f(x + \phi) = f(x + \phi + T) $$

Fourier series

The idea of Fourier series is that every periodic function \( f \) with a base frequency of \(f_1\) can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoidals with integer multiples of that frequency and variable phase and magnitude. $$ f(t) = A_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin(2\pi f_nt + \phi_n) $$ Where $ f_n = n f_1 $. An alternative form of the sum is of non shifted sines and cosines, because of the addition formula for sines. \begin{align} &A_n\sin(2\pi f_nt + \phi_n) \\ & = A_n\cos(\phi_n) \sin(2\pi f_nt) + A_n\sin(\phi_n) \cos(2\pi f_nt) \\ & = S_n \sin(2\pi f_nt) + C_n \cos(2\pi f_nt) \end{align}
